How inequality is experienced in cities depends on who people encounter, where they go, and when activities occur—dimensions that no single urban dataset captures jointly. As a result, key aspects of social exposure, access to opportunity, and participation in daily life remain difficult to measure at population scale. Household travel surveys provide rich demographic and behavioral detail but are sparse in space and time, whereas passively collected mobility data capture near-complete origin–destination flows but lack information on activity purpose and individual characteristics. Here, we develop a privacy-preserving data-fusion framework to reconstruct population-scale daily activity schedules by integrating these complementary sources. The approach combines the behavioral richness of survey data with the spatiotemporal completeness of passive data, enabling joint resolution of who participates, where and when activities occur, how long they last, and for what purpose—without requiring individual-level trajectories. Applied to Singapore (${\sim}5$ million residents) and Seoul (${\sim}8$ million residents), the reconstructed schedules expand observed activity-pattern diversity by two to three orders of magnitude and are validated against independent smart-card and cellular mobility datasets not used in estimation.
Using these schedules, we quantify three dimensions of experienced mobility inequality—social exposure, spatial opportunity, and temporal participation—across income, age, and gender. Activity-based segregation reveals purpose-specific patterns that are invisible to conventional co-presence metrics. Spatial opportunity exhibits a gender-dependent trade-off between activity-space diversity and extent. Temporal participation disparities arise through both time-budget reallocation and schedule reorganization. Across all dimensions, survey-only approaches overestimate inequality and produce unstable patterns, while aggregation that ignores activity purpose obscures underlying mechanisms and can even reverse the inferred direction of income inequality.
These results show that credible measurement of experienced mobility inequality requires jointly resolving demographic attributes, activity semantics, and spatiotemporal structure at population scale.
